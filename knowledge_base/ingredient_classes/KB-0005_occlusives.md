# Summary

Occlusives are ingredients that reduce transepidermal water loss (TEWL) by forming a protective layer on the skin surface.

They are one of the primary mechanisms used in moisturization and barrier-support skincare products.

Occlusives are particularly important for users experiencing dehydration, barrier dysfunction, dryness, or irritation.

# Scientific Background

Water continuously evaporates from the skin through a process known as Trans-Epidermal Water Loss (TEWL).

Occlusive ingredients help reduce this water loss by creating a protective barrier on the skin surface.

Different occlusives vary in effectiveness, texture, cosmetic elegance, and user acceptance.

# Common Examples

* Petrolatum
* Mineral Oil
* Dimethicone
* Lanolin
* Beeswax

# Why It Matters

Occlusives may help:

* Reduce TEWL
* Improve skin comfort
* Support barrier recovery
* Reduce dryness
* Improve moisturizer performance

They are particularly valuable when barrier function is compromised.

# Routine Implications

Occlusives are commonly found in:

* Moisturizers
* Barrier repair products
* Healing ointments

They are often most effective when used alongside humectants and emollients.

# Safety Notes

Most common occlusives have low irritation potential.

Texture preferences may influence adherence and user satisfaction.

Some users with oily skin may dislike highly occlusive textures, although this does not necessarily indicate incompatibility.

# Common Misconceptions

## "Occlusives prevent skin from breathing."

False.

Modern cosmetic occlusives reduce water loss but do not completely seal the skin.

## "Occlusives cause dehydration."

False.

Their primary function is to reduce water loss.

## "All oils are occlusives."

False.

Many oils function primarily as emollients.

# Decision Rules

If a user reports:

* Dryness
* Tightness
* Flaking
* Barrier damage

AYNA should consider products containing appropriate occlusive ingredients as part of a hydration and barrier-support strategy.
